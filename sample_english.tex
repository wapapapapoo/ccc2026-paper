\title{Differentiable Superpixel Embedding with\\Geometry-Aware Token Representation}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Superpixels provide content-aligned visual primitives for token-based vision models, forming a compact representation suitable for structured perception.
However, most existing approaches encode superpixels using hard region assignments or fixed statistical descriptors, which restrict their ability to capture intrinsic spatial structure.
We propose a differentiable superpixel embedding framework that learns token representations directly from the soft pixel-to-superpixel affinity matrix, modeling each superpixel as a continuous spatial distribution with overlapping support.
The embedding is decomposed into appearance, geometry, and positional components.
A dedicated geometry encoder captures superpixel shape and scale directly from affinity distributions using a seed-centered reassembly operation followed by a lightweight convolutional network.
The resulting tokens preserve appearance semantics while incorporating explicit geometric structure.
Experiments on semantic segmentation tasks demonstrate consistent performance gains over existing superpixel embedding strategies.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{superpixel, semantic segmentation, deep learning}

\section{Introduction}

Semantic segmentation is a fundamental dense prediction task that requires accurate pixel-level classification while maintaining coherent scene-level understanding.
A central challenge is to capture global, long-range dependencies across complex scenes while preserving precise local boundary delineation.
Existing approaches employ relational reasoning frameworks, such as Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and Transformers, to model global interactions \cite{chen2024survey}.
However, these models rely on structured intermediate tokens.
Fixed $n \times n$ patch tokenization, as adopted in Vision Transformers (ViT) \cite{vit} and SegFormer \cite{segformer}, offers a simple formulation but disregards object boundaries and often mixes semantically inconsistent regions \cite{shao2024homogeneous,lu2024spenhancevit}.
Superpixels provide a content-adaptive alternative by grouping perceptually similar and spatially proximal pixels into boundary-aware regions, forming semantically coherent primitives for downstream inference.

Superpixels are generated by clustering pixels with similar appearance and spatial proximity into oversegmented regions \cite{slic}.
These regions inherently possess irregular shapes and varying scales.
Such geometric variability is not naturally compatible with the fixed-dimensional vector representations expected by downstream relational models.
Transforming irregular superpixels into unified and geometry-aware token representations remains a non-trivial problem in superpixel-based segmentation pipelines, as illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:method-function}.

    \label{fig:method-function}
\end{figure}

However, most existing approaches treat superpixels as discrete, non-overlapping regions and encode them using either fixed statistical descriptors (e.g., mean color, texture histograms, or spatial coordinates) \cite{spit} or features averaged during an offline preprocessing stage \cite{spgcn}.
In dense prediction tasks, this design leads to two limitations.
First, hard region assignments discard the overlapping spatial support intrinsic to convolutional feature representations, thereby increasing sensitivity to segmentation errors and boundary artifacts.
Second, summarizing irregular regions with simple first-order statistics limits the ability to capture internal spatial configuration and geometric structure.
Consequently, such embeddings remain weakly aligned with downstream objectives and cannot be jointly optimized with end-task supervision.

In this paper, we reinterpret superpixel tokens as continuous spatial distributions rather than discrete regions represented by pooled appearance statistics.
By modeling each superpixel through its soft pixel-to-superpixel affinity field, the proposed formulation retains its continuous support and internal structure, rather than reducing it to statistical descriptors.
In addition to aggregated appearance features, we explicitly encode the intrinsic geometry implied by the affinity distribution, resulting in a differentiable and geometry-aware superpixel representation that can be jointly optimized with downstream models.

To isolate the effect of the embedding design, we adopt simplified superpixel-based pipelines with either a GNN or a Transformer as the downstream model.
Experiments on the SBD dataset demonstrate performance gains over baseline embedding strategies, confirming that carefully designed superpixel representations substantially influence the effectiveness of region-based dense prediction models.

Our contributions are summarized as follows:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Continuous and differentiable superpixel tokenization: }
We model superpixels as soft affinity-based distributions rather than hard region assignments.
This formulation produces overlapping tokens with continuous support, mitigating boundary artifacts and preserving spatial coherence.

\item \textbf{Affinity-driven geometric encoding: }
We introduce a geometry encoder with a seed-centered affinity reassembly operation and a lightweight CNN.
The encoder captures intrinsic superpixel shape and scale directly from the affinity field, embedding geometric structure into compact feature representations.

\item \textbf{Geometry-aware token fusion mechanism: }
We develop a gated fusion module that integrates appearance, geometric, and positional features within a unified superpixel encoder.
The FiLM-style gating mechanism modulates appearance representations using structural cues, enabling task-adaptive and geometry-aware superpixel tokens.

\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Token-based Models for Vision Tasks}

Vision Transformers (ViT) \cite{vit} reformulate images as sequences of patch tokens and employ standard Transformer encoders for visual recognition.
They demonstrate that global self-attention over patch tokens can achieve performance comparable to or exceeding that of convolutional neural networks.
Subsequent studies, such as Swin Transformer \cite{swin}, improve computational efficiency and scalability through hierarchical token representations and adaptive token pruning strategies.

Beyond token construction, various relational reasoning mechanisms have been proposed to model interactions among visual tokens.
Vision GNN (ViG) \cite{vig} demonstrates that graph-based message passing over visual tokens provides an effective alternative to self-attention.
SegFormer \cite{segformer} further shows that lightweight Transformer-based inference combined with multi-scale feature aggregation achieves competitive performance on dense prediction tasks.

\subsection{Soft-Assigned Superpixel Segmentation}

Many superpixel-based pipelines (e.g. \cite{wei2025dual}) rely on offline superpixel generation followed by fixed node embeddings, primarily due to the non-differentiability of the classical Simple Linear Iterative Clustering (SLIC) \cite{slic} algorithm.
This limitation is addressed by recent differentiable superpixel segmentation methods based on soft assignments.
Superpixel Sampling Network (SSN) \cite{ssn} reformulates SLIC as a differentiable clustering procedure, enabling end-to-end gradient propagation.
Superpixel Fully Convolutional Network (SpixelFCN) \cite{sfcn} predicts superpixel assignments directly in the forward pass.
These methods produce a soft assignment tensor $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times H \times W}$, where $Q(:, h, w)$ represents the affinities between the pixel at location $(h, w)$ and its $k$ neighboring superpixel seeds.
In practice, $k$ typically corresponds to the $3 \times 3$ neighborhood of superpixel seeds around each pixel on a regular $16 \times 16$ seed grid, yielding at most nine affinities per pixel.

\subsection{Superpixel Embedding Methods}

A central design choice in superpixel-based pipelines is the embedding strategy used to convert superpixels into feature vectors for relational reasoning.
Early approaches, such as SPGCN \cite{spgcn}, compute superpixel embeddings by averaging pixel-level features within each region as a preprocessing step.
The node features remain fixed throughout training.

SPiT \cite{spit} is among the first works to systematically analyze the information that a superpixel embedding should encode.
It represents each superpixel using five factors: color, texture, shape, scale, and position.
However, SPiT relies on hand-crafted feature extractors, while effective, this design does not yield task-adaptive representations.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Overview}

We focus on the design of superpixel token representations for dense prediction.
Building upon the factorization analysis introduced in SPiT~\cite{spit}, we model the five superpixel factors using fully differentiable modules, enabling end-to-end optimization of superpixel tokens with downstream models.

Given an input image $X \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times H \times W}$, a soft superpixel segmentation method (e.g., differentiable SLIC proposed in SSN~\cite{ssn}, SpixelFCN~\cite{sfcn}) predicts a pixel-to-superpixel affinity tensor $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{9 \times H \times W}$, where each pixel is softly assigned to its $3 \times 3$ neighboring seeds on a regular grid with spacing $S$. The total number of superpixels is $N = \frac{H}{S} \cdot \frac{W}{S}$.
Based on $Q$ and pixel-level features, we construct a token embedding $\mathbf{z}_s$ for each superpixel seed $s$.

As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:mainfig}, we divide the five factors of a superpixel into three branches: \textbf{appearance}, \textbf{geometry}, and \textbf{position}, and embed them using different strategies.

\textbf{The appearance branch encodes color and texture.}
Color is a per-pixel attribute, while texture corresponds to local high-frequency patterns that can be captured by a small number of convolutional layers.
We therefore use a lightweight CNN to extract pixel features $F$ and obtain superpixel appearance features via soft weighted pooling weighted by the affinity $Q$.

\textbf{The geometry branch encodes superpixel shape and scale.}
In our embedding formulation, these geometric properties do not depend on pixel-level appearance features and do not explicitly encode absolute spatial position; instead, they are determined by the spatial support of the superpixel itself.
We apply a seed-centered affinity reassembly operation that rearranges the affinity matrix $Q$ in each seed-centered neighborhood area and produces a local affinity tensor $Q' \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times 3S \times 3S}$.
Each slice $Q'(s,:,:)$ represents the continuous spatial support of superpixel $s$ in a bounded local region.
This representation is then embedded with a lightweight convolutional geometry encoder.

\textbf{The positional branch encodes superpixel location.}
We compute a soft centroid for each superpixel by aggregating normalized pixel coordinates weighted by $Q$, and embed it using a small MLP.
As discussed in \cite{segformer}, absolute positional information improves spatial awareness but introduces a trade-off with translation invariance and generalization.
We therefore treat position as an auxiliary signal and avoid directly entangling it with appearance features.

These three branches are finally combined through a superpixel encoder.
The geometry and positional embeddings modulate the appearance features via a FiLM mechanism, producing the final superpixel token feature vector $\mathbf{z}_s$.
This design preserves appearance semantics while incorporating geometric and spatial features without overriding them.

\subsection{Geometric Branch with a Convolutional Encoder}

The geometric branch encodes superpixel-intrinsic structure while remaining invariant to appearance and absolute image location.
To achieve this, we first transform the affinity responses into a canonical, seed-aligned representation, and then embed it using a convolutional geometry encoder.

As illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:saro}, we introduce a seed-centered affinity reassembly operation that reorganizes the affinity responses of each superpixel into a local coordinate frame centered at each seed.
For a given seed, affinity contributions from its neighboring seeds are spatially shifted and reassembled such that the seed of interest occupies the center of the representation.
This results in a spatially continuous, fixed-size 2D affinity map that describes the spatial support of the superpixel relative to its seed.
The procedure is summarized in \textbf{Algorithm~\ref{alg:q2qprime}}.

\begin{algorithm}[!t]
\caption{Seed-centered affinity reassembly}
\label{alg:q2qprime}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]

\REQUIRE 
    affinity tensor $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{9 \times H \times W}$; seed spacing $S$
\ENSURE 
    seed-centered affinity maps $Q' = \{Q'_s\}_{s=1}^{N}$

\STATE $W_s \gets \frac{W}{S},~H_s \gets \frac{H}{S},~N \gets W_s \cdot H_s$

\STATE $Q^{\mathrm{pad}} \gets \mathrm{Pad}(Q,\; (S,S,S,S))$

\STATE $\mathcal{O} \gets \big[(1, 1), (1, 0), \dots, (-1, -1)\big]$

\FOR{$j = 1$ to $9$}
    \STATE $(\Delta_x,\Delta_y) \gets \mathcal{O}(j)$
    \STATE $x_0 \gets S(1+\Delta_x),~y_0 \gets S(1+\Delta_y)$
    \STATE $\widetilde{Q}(j,:,:) \gets Q^{\mathrm{pad}}(j,\; y_0:y_0+H,\; x_0:x_0+W)$
\ENDFOR

\FOR{$s_y = 0$ to $H_s-1$}
    \FOR{$s_x = 0$ to $W_s-1$}
        \STATE $s \gets s_y \cdot W_s + s_x + 1$
        \STATE Initialize $Q'_s \gets \mathbf{0}_{3S \times 3S}$
        \STATE $\widetilde{Q}_s \gets \widetilde{Q}(:,\; s_y \cdot S:(s_y+1) \cdot S,\; s_x \cdot S:(s_x+1) \cdot S)$
        \FOR{$j = 1$ to $9$}
            \STATE $(\Delta_x,\Delta_y) \gets \mathcal{O}(j)$
            \STATE $R_y \gets (\Delta_y+1) \cdot S:(\Delta_y+2) \cdot S$ 
            \STATE $R_x \gets (\Delta_x+1) \cdot S:(\Delta_x+2) \cdot S$
            \STATE $Q'_s(R_y, R_x) \gets \widetilde{Q}_s(j,:,:)$

        \ENDFOR
    \ENDFOR
\ENDFOR

\RETURN $Q'$

\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:sla-exam}, after the reassembly operation, each superpixel is associated with a seed-centered affinity map $Q'_s = Q'(s,:,:) \in \mathbb{R}^{3S \times 3S}$, which provides a geometry-only description independent of global image coordinates.
Each seed-centered affinity map $Q'_s$ is treated as a discrete, non-negative scalar field defined on a seed-aligned local domain $\Omega_{\mathrm{loc}}$ of size $3S\times 3S$.
\begin{equation}
Q'_s : \Omega_{\mathrm{loc}} \subset \mathbb{R}^2 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}
\end{equation}
represents a soft superpixel occupancy distribution defined on a local, seed-aligned coordinate frame.
We embed this field using the geometry encoder.
Each $Q'_s$ is processed independently as a single-channel input by a stack of strided convolutional layers with normalization and nonlinearity, denoted by $\phi(\cdot)$, followed by global average pooling (GAP):
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}_s = \mathrm{GAP}\!\left(\phi(Q'_s)\right).
\end{equation}
Here $\mathbf{h}_s \in \mathbb{R}^d$, where $d$ is the output channel dimension of the geometry encoder.

This CNN-GAP structure performs spatial aggregation over the occupancy field.
Equivalently, the learned convolutional filters implicitly parameterize a set of geometric basis functions $\{\varphi_k\}_{k=1}^d$ over $\Omega_{\mathrm{loc}}$, and each channel of $\mathbf{h}_s$ approximates the inner product
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}_{s,k} \;\approx\; \langle Q'_s, \varphi_k \rangle
\;=\;
\sum_{p \in \Omega_{\mathrm{loc}}} Q'_s(p)\,\varphi_k(p).
\end{equation}
As a result, $\mathbf{h}_s$ represents the superpixel geometry as coefficients in a learned low-dimensional shape space, where different channels respond to characteristic spatial patterns of the soft occupancy field, such as elongation, anisotropy, or boundary bias.

To explicitly preserve scale information that may be attenuated by convolution and pooling, we compute the total mass of the occupancy field,
\begin{equation}
a_s = \sum_{p \in \Omega_{\mathrm{loc}}} Q'_s(p),
\end{equation}
which corresponds to its zeroth-order moment and reflects the effective size of the superpixel.
This scalar is projected into the embedding space by a learnable mapping $\psi(\cdot)$,
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{e}_s = \psi(a_s),
\end{equation}
and combined additively with the shape coefficients:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z}_s^{\mathrm{geo}} = \mathbf{h}_s + \mathbf{e}_s.
\end{equation}

The resulting geometry embedding $\mathbf{z}_s^{\mathrm{geo}}$ therefore encodes both superpixel shape and scale, is computed solely from affinity geometry, and is independent of appearance features and global image coordinates.
It serves as a compact geometric descriptor for subsequent feature fusion.

\subsection{Superpixel Encoder with Gated Fusion}

With appearance, geometry, and positional features extracted for each superpixel, we now describe how these factors are fused into a unified token representation.
Let $\Omega_{\mathrm{img}}$ denote the global image domain (all pixels). We define $Q_s(p)$ as the affinity from pixel location $p$ to seed $s$, obtained from the 9-channel tensor $Q$ via the neighbor ordering $\mathcal{O}$ in Algorithm~\ref{alg:q2qprime} (and $Q_s(p)=0$ if $s$ is not among the 9 neighbors). 
Given pixel features $F \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times H \times W}$ and the soft assignment tensor $Q$, we first define the mass of superpixel $s$ as
\begin{equation}
m_s = \sum_{p \in \Omega_{\mathrm{img}}} Q_s(p).
\end{equation}
The appearance embedding is then obtained by soft average pooling,
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z}_s^{\mathrm{app}}
=
\frac{1}{m_s}
\sum_{p \in \Omega_{\mathrm{img}}} Q_s(p)\,F(p).
\end{equation}

The spatial location of each superpixel is encoded independently of appearance by computing a soft centroid over normalized coordinates $P(p) = (\frac{p_x}{W}, \frac{p_y}{H})$, which is embedded using a lightweight MLP $g$,
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z}_s^{\mathrm{pos}} = g(
    \frac{1}{m_s}
    \sum_{p \in \Omega_{\mathrm{img}}} Q_s(p)\,P(p)
).
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[!tb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{img/fusion/fusion.pdf}
    \caption{The FiLM-Style fusion module.}
    \label{fig:fusion}
\end{figure}

The final superpixel token is constructed via gated fusion demonstrated in Fig.\ref{fig:fusion}.
Geometry and positional embeds are concated to generate channel-wise modulation parameters,
\begin{equation}
(\boldsymbol{\gamma}_s, \boldsymbol{\beta}_s)
=
\mathrm{MLP}\!\left([\mathbf{z}_s^{\mathrm{geo}}, \mathbf{z}_s^{\mathrm{pos}}]\right),
\end{equation}
allowing the model to adaptively regulate the influence of absolute positional information for each superpixel.
These parameters modulate the appearance embedding,
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z}_s
=
\mathrm{MLP}\!\left(\mathbf{z}_s^{\mathrm{app}} \odot (1 + \boldsymbol{\gamma}_s) + \boldsymbol{\beta}_s\right) .
\end{equation}

This FiLM-style gating allows geometry and position to influence appearance features while preserving their semantic content, resulting in geometry-aware superpixel tokens.

\section{Experiment}

\subsection{Implementation Detail and Experiment Setup}

To evaluate the proposed superpixel embedding method, we adopt two lightweight pipelines, each consisting of a frozen SpixelFCN as the upstream superpixel generator, a lightweight CNN for pixel feature extraction, and different downstream models: a four-layer topology-adaptive graph convolution network (TAGCN) \cite{tagcn}, and a ViT-style model composed of four Transformer encoder layers with four attention heads.
The total number of parameters across all evaluated models ranges from 6.7M to 7.9M, while the proposed embedder contains 172K parameters.
At a resolution of $384\times512$, the computational cost of our embedder is 1.79 GFLOPs per forward pass, while Its asymptotic time complexity is $\mathcal{O}(BHWC_1+BNC_2)$, where $C_1$ and $C_2$ are constants, maintaining a lightweight computational footprint.

% param size: GeoEncoder=14272, PosEmbed=320, Fusion=157184; sfcn=2287956, enc=3271936, transformer=3159552, decoder=1268054, dslic=14714688

All experiments are conducted on the Semantic Boundaries Dataset (SBD)~\cite{sbd}, an augmented dataset based on PASCAL VOC 2011 that provides pixel-level annotations for additional VOC 2011 images without segmentation labels.
The dataset contains 11,318 images, split into 8,498 training and 2,820 validation samples.
Compared to the original VOC annotations, SBD labels are relatively coarse.
All images are randomly cropped to a size of $384\times512$.
We report mean Intersection-over-Union (mIoU) as the evaluation metric.

\subsection{Results and Ablation Study}

Table~\ref{tab:main_result} reports segmentation results obtained using different superpixel embedding strategies with TAGCN and Transformer downstream models.
The proposed geometry-aware embedding with soft assignment yields the best performance in both cases, achieving 79.28\% mIoU with TAGCN and 81.06\% with the Transformer.
Comparing Row 1 and Row 3 shows that our method obviously outperforms the SPiT-style embedding under GNN setting (79.28\% vs. 74.41\%) with a comparable number of parameters.
This comparison indicates that learnable embeddings is more effective than hand-craft statistical descriptors for representing superpixels.

Replacing the soft affinity with hard labels obtained by $\arg\max Q$ leads to consistent performance degradation.
Under TAGCN, mIoU drops from 79.28\% to 77.05\%; under the Transformer, it drops from 81.06\% to 78.31\%.
The decline across both architectures demonstrates that soft, overlapping assignments are superior to hard partitioning.
Modeling superpixels as continuous spatial distributions provides a more informative representation than treating them as fixed regions.

The ablation study on the geometry and position branches further clarifies their roles.
Removing both branches reduces mIoU to 75.35\%, showing that appearance-only aggregation is insufficient.
When only the positional branch is removed, performance improves to 77.09\%, indicating that positional encoding contributes complementary information but is not the primary factor.
The larger drop associated with removing geometry confirms that encoding intrinsic shape and scale is critical.
Overall, these results show that explicitly embedding superpixel geometric and spatial structure leads to measurable gains.
Despite the lightweight experimental setup, the consistent improvements support the effectiveness of the proposed geometry-aware superpixel embedding.

We further conduct a KNN-based auxiliary experiment to directly assess whether the learned superpixel embeddings encode task-relevant semantic information.
A model trained on SBD is applied to the training split to extract superpixel embeddings $\mathbf{z}_s$.
For each superpixel, a soft semantic label distribution $\mathbf{y}_s$ is obtained via weighted average pooling of pixel-level one-hot ground truth, forming key-value pairs $\langle \mathbf{z}_s, \mathbf{y}_s \rangle$ stored in memory.
During validation, superpixel embeds are used as queries to retrieve their $k$ nearest neighbors from this set.
The neighbors vote to determine the semantic category of each query superpixel, and aggregating all superpixel predictions produces the final segmentation map.

Experiment results are reported in Table~\ref{tab:knn_embed_eval}.
The SPiT-like embedding exhibits performance close to the noise baseline under this evaluation protocol, suggesting that it captures lower-level or less semantic information.
In contrast, the proposed embedding yields substantially higher accuracy and mIoU, indicating that it encodes discriminative, task-aligned semantic representations that can be directly exploited through non-parametric retrieval.

\section*{CONCLUSION}

In this paper, we introduced a geometry-aware superpixel embedding framework that models each superpixel as a continuous spatial distribution and explicitly encodes its intrinsic shape and scale based on pixel-to-superpixel affinities.
Experiments on semantic segmentation benchmarks demonstrate that the proposed embedding yields more discriminative superpixel representations and consistently improves the performance of superpixel-based segmentation pipelines.
The results further indicate that effective superpixel embeddings should exploit the affinity distributions produced by differentiable superpixel segmentation, rather than relying on hand-crafted geometric descriptors or purely appearance-based aggregation.
